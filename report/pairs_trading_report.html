Pairs Trading Strategy Using Cointegration¶

This notebook presents a cointegration-based pairs trading strategy across S&P 500. The goal is to identify cointegrated stock pairs and use a mean-reversion strategy to exploit the arbitrage, and evaluate whether the strategy survives out of sample validation.

We found that the strategy produces strong results in the in-sample period, with a Sharpe ratio of 2.07, but performs significantly worse in the test period, with a Sharpe ratio of only 0.78. This decay is likely attributed to overfitting during pair selection and parameter tuning, as well as structural changes in stock relationships over time. Because pairs are selected using historical relationships from the training period, some of these relationships may weaken or break down in later market regimes.

2. Data and Universe¶

The universe is constructed by first retrieving the current S&P 500 stocks and download the historical data via yfinance from 2014-01-01 to 2025-12-31. The final cleaned data is split into train, validation, and test periods: train from 2014 to 2019, validation in 2020, and test from 2021 to 2025. We then remove stocks with short histories or excessive missing observations.

3. Pair Discovery¶

Pairs are discovered using the Engle-Granger cointegration test on log prices. The hedge ratio is estimated using OLS, and the residual spread is tested with ADF. Pairs are ranked by cointegration p-value, ADF p-value, and half-life.

4. Signal Construction¶

The spread is defined as:

$$spread_t = log(P_y)_t - alpha - beta \cdot log(P_x)_t$$

The z-score normalizes the spread using a rolling mean and rolling standard deviation. Signals are lagged by one day so that trades only use observable information. We enter a position for a pair once its z-score exceeds the entry threshold and hold that position until the z-score falls below the exit threshold or exceeds the stop-loss cap.

8. Conclusion and Limitations¶

The cointegration-based pair trading strategy exhibits a very strong performance during the in-sample period, but it decays during the out-of-sample period, which is likely attributed to overfitting during pair selection and parameter tuning and structural changes in stock relationships over time.

There are several limitations associated with the project. The universe is based on the current S&P 500 constituents, so the backtest excludes firms that were removed from the index during the sample period. This leads to survivorship bias that may make results look better than in real time. Additionally, we use a static hedge ratio, but the relationship between two stocks can change over time, so a rolling beta may be more realistic.
